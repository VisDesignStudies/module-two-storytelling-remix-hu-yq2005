Too Many Cafes? Understanding South Korea’s Coffee Boom

A remix of the New York Times story, expanding the question from oversaturation to culture, labor, and entrepreneurship.

The original article frames South Korea’s cafe explosion as a market problem: too many shops competing in too little space. That interpretation is persuasive — and still incomplete. This remix adds a second reading: the boom can also reflect strong demand, a deeply embedded coffee culture, and labor-market pressure that makes small business ownership feel like a rational (if risky) path.

Section 1 · Demand

A Nation of Coffee Drinkers

The coffee boom begins not with entrepreneurs, but with consumers. If many people are opening cafes, part of the explanation lies in how deeply coffee has become integrated into everyday life. The argument here is not that consumption alone explains the growth of cafes. Rather, it establishes the foundation of the market: coffee in South Korea is not an occasional indulgence but a daily routine.

Coffee functions as more than a beverage. It is a social ritual, a meeting place, and often a companion to work or study. When demand becomes habitual, the expanding number of cafes appears less mysterious and more like a market responding to everyday behavior.

Section 2 · Expansion

The Rapid Growth of Cafes

Demand created the conditions for growth. The next step was the remarkable expansion of cafes themselves. This visualization highlights the overall shape of the boom. The number of coffee shops increased sharply through the late 2010s and into the early 2020s, nearly doubling within a relatively short period.

The takeaway is straightforward: the market expanded rapidly. This makes the New York Times concern about saturation understandable. Yet it still leaves an important question unresolved — why did new businesses continue entering the market even as competition intensified?

Section 3 · Pressure

Why Do So Many People Keep Opening Cafes?

The persistence of new cafe openings can seem puzzling if we view the decision purely as a calculation of profit and competition. A different explanation may lie in economic pressure — and how individuals adapt to it.

Opening a cafe can appear to be an accessible form of entrepreneurship. Coffee is a familiar product, the business model is visible in everyday life, and cafes themselves are culturally legible spaces for work, study, and social interaction.

This does not mean unemployment directly caused the growth of cafes. Rather, unemployment may act as a background condition that helps explain why small-business entry remains understandable. In an environment where stable employment can feel uncertain, entrepreneurship may appear to be one of the few visible alternatives.

Even as the number of coffee shops passed 100,000, unemployment remained part of the economic context shaping how people navigated opportunity and risk.

Section 4 · Counterargument

Is the Coffee Boom Really a Problem?

The New York Times article frames the cafe surge primarily as a warning sign: too many businesses competing for limited customers. That risk is real, and many small businesses may struggle to survive.

Yet this perspective does not capture the entire picture.

If the boom were purely the result of oversupply, we might expect cafe growth to diverge from underlying demand. Instead, two demand-side indicators moved in the same direction: coffee consumption increased, and the market value of the cafe industry expanded.

These signals do not guarantee success for every individual cafe. They do, however, complicate the story of saturation. The coffee boom can be understood both as a sign of intense competition and as evidence of a growing consumer market.

Section 5 · Conclusion

A Boom With More Than One Cause

South Korea’s coffee boom cannot be explained by a single narrative.

Market saturation and fierce competition are real challenges. At the same time, the boom also reflects strong consumer demand, a deeply rooted coffee culture, and the economic pressures that can make entrepreneurship appear attractive.

This remix expands the framing offered in the original New York Times article. Rather than viewing the cafe explosion only as a market warning, it can also be interpreted as a form of economic adaptation — a response to cultural habits, market opportunities, and labor-market realities.
